## The standard questions (most likely to be asked — know these cold)

### "What is SpeakBold, in one sentence?"
> A free app that makes you better at speaking — interviews, presentations, debates, pitches — by having you actually speak and telling you exactly what to fix, every time.

### "What problem are you solving, and for whom?"
> Speaking well decides interviews, pitches, and presentations — but practice with real feedback costs ~RM150/session with a coach, which students and jobseekers can't afford. So most people get zero reps before the moment that counts. We give them unlimited reps, with specific feedback, for free.

### "Walk me through how it works." (the 30-second version)
> You pick a drill — interview answer, impromptu topic, debate motion — you speak into your phone, and in seconds you get scored feedback on structure, clarity, pacing, and persuasion: what worked, what to fix next. Optional camera mode adds body-language feedback, processed entirely on your own device. And if you want stakes, the Arena puts you in live ranked battles against an AI or a real person.

### "What's your traction?"
> 81 registered learners, 504 completed drills — about 6 per user, so they come back — 74% start the curriculum, all with zero marketing. These are live numbers from our production database; I can pull them right now in front of you.

### "How do you make money?" / "What's the business model?"
> Free for learners, forever — that's the mission. Revenue comes from institutions: schools and universities licensing teacher dashboards for debate clubs, language departments, career centres. The institution pays; the student never does.

### "Who are your competitors and why are you better?"
> Yoodli, Orai, Poised. Three differences: they're feedback tools, we're also a game — live PvP debate with a ranking ladder doesn't exist anywhere else; our body-language analysis runs on your device, theirs uploads to the cloud; and they're paid Western products — we're free and built for our market.

### "How big is the market?"
> Every student and jobseeker who'll face an interview — in Malaysia alone that's hundreds of thousands of graduates a year, before counting schools. But honestly, the more useful frame: the market for speaking practice was always huge and the product was always RM150/session. We didn't find a new market; we removed its price tag.

### "Why will users keep coming back?"
> Four hooks: streaks and daily challenges for habit, the ELO ladder and rank emblems for competition, friends comparisons for social pressure, and the Growth Report for visible payoff. At small scale it's working — ~6 drills per user is return behaviour, not curiosity.

### "How do you know people actually improve?"
> Your first recording becomes your permanent baseline, and the Growth Report charts every skill against it — your own voice, then and now. Our cohort is young, so most users are early in that curve — but the measurement is built and live. We show growth, we don't assert it.

### "What about privacy?" (plain version)
> Your voice recordings are private to you and deletable. The camera is the stronger promise: body-language analysis runs entirely on your own device — no video frame is ever uploaded, not even to us. And if you're camera-shy, there's a preview mode that shows how it works without turning your camera on at all.

### "Is this just a ChatGPT wrapper?" (plain version)
> No — the AI scoring is one part of a bigger system: speech transcription, on-device body-language tracking, a structured curriculum, a server-controlled rating system, and live two-player debates. Swap out any one AI provider and SpeakBold still works — we run four, with automatic failover.

### "Is the leaderboard real?" ⚠️ know this one
> The traction numbers — 81 users, 504 drills — are 100% real. The public leaderboard additionally includes seeded demo profiles so the ladder shows what it looks like at scale, the same way a beta product shows sample data before its community fills in. Those accounts are clearly tagged on our side and can't log in. Real users and their activity are exactly as reported.

### "What's next on the roadmap?"
> Three things: teacher dashboards so one educator can run a whole class through the curriculum; tournament mode for inter-school debate events; and over time, scoring models tuned on the rubric-scored speech data we're accumulating — which nobody else is collecting.

### "What was your biggest mistake during development?"
> Our first onboarding was a 15-screen tour almost nobody finished — people gave up before ever speaking once. We cut it to "pick a goal, do your first drill." The lesson: for a speaking product, the fastest path to value is making someone speak, not explaining the product to them.

### "How would a school actually adopt this?"
> Today, a teacher hands students a URL — no install, no IT approval, everyone's practising in five minutes. The roadmap piece is the dashboard that lets that teacher assign drills and watch the class's growth reports. That's the paid layer, and it's also the distribution strategy.

## The "why does this deserve to win" cluster

### "Why should this win, in one sentence?"
> Because it takes something that was RM150 a session — useful feedback on how you speak — and makes it free for anyone with a browser, and it's not a concept: it's live, with 81 real users and 504 completed sessions, zero marketing.

### "Why hasn't this been solved already?"
> Until very recently it *couldn't* be — you needed a human to listen, judge, and respond, and humans don't scale and aren't free. The AI capability that makes a tireless, consistent speaking judge possible is about two years old. We're early on a problem that just became solvable, which is exactly when you want to be building.

### "What's your unfair advantage? Anyone could build this."
> Anyone could build a demo. What's hard to copy is what we've already accumulated: a working curriculum across five speaking formats, a judging rubric refined against hundreds of real sessions, a competitive ladder that makes practice addictive, and real users improving on it right now. The advantage isn't a secret — it's a head start plus the willingness to keep it free, which a profit-first competitor structurally can't match.

### "What happens to this after the competition?"
> It doesn't need the competition to exist — it's already deployed and people already use it without us asking. Win or lose, the next step is the same: get it in front of one school's debate club or career centre as a pilot, and let teachers tell us what the educator dashboard needs to look like.

### "If you win, what do you spend the prize on?"
> Three things, in order: API and infrastructure headroom so we can serve a school-sized cohort without throttling; a small pilot with one real classroom — the cost is mostly our time and transport, not money; and keeping the product free while we do it. None of it goes to marketing — the product growing organically is the proof we'd be spending to protect.

## The AI-skeptic cluster

### "Can an AI really replace a human speaking coach?"
> It doesn't have to replace the best human coach — it has to beat the realistic alternative, which for most students is *no coach at all*. Against nothing, specific feedback in thirty seconds, available at 2am, unlimited retries, zero judgment — that wins. And for the minority who do have a human coach, this is what they practise with between sessions.

### "Doesn't speaking need a real audience? You can't simulate stage fright."
> True — and we don't claim to. What we fix is the step *before* the audience: most people walk on stage having never once heard themselves structured, timed, and critiqued. The Arena adds real stakes — a live human opponent, a rating that can drop — which is genuinely uncomfortable in a useful way. We're the flight simulator. You still have to fly the plane, but nobody argues pilots should skip the simulator.

### "Won't AI feedback make everyone speak the same way?"
> The rubric scores structure, clarity, pacing, persuasion — qualities every great speaker shares while sounding nothing alike. We never score "sound like X." Churchill and a stand-up comedian would both score well, for different reasons. If anything, the bigger homogenizer is having *no* feedback — people just imitate whoever they saw last.

### "Aren't you making kids dependent on AI?"
> The skill being trained lives entirely in the user — when you stand up in an interview, the app isn't there, but the pacing, the structure, the confidence are. That's the opposite of dependency tools like AI essay writers, where the output *is* the AI's. We're closer to a treadmill than a crutch: the machine is in the loop during training and absent during performance.

### "How do you know the feedback is actually good and not just plausible-sounding?"
> Three honest answers. The rubric is fixed, so feedback has to point at something specific — your structure, your pacing — not vibes. Users behave like it's useful: ~6 sessions per user means they came back after seeing the feedback, which they wouldn't for fluff. And the Growth Report makes it falsifiable — if scores didn't move with practice, we'd see it. What we haven't done is a formal study against human-coach ratings; that's a pilot-stage step, and we'd genuinely like to run it.

## The impact / mission cluster

### "How does this serve SDG-4, concretely?"
> SDG-4 is quality education for *all* — and speaking is the most economically consequential skill that school almost never formally teaches. Today it's taught by money: private coaches, expensive schools, Toastmasters in cities. We collapsed the cost of useful practice to zero and the access requirement to "a browser." That's not adjacent to SDG-4, it's the definition of it.

### "How do you reach the students who need this most — rural, low-income?"
> The product is already shaped for them: no install, no fee, works on a modest phone, drills as short as 30 seconds so it fits between obligations. What's missing is distribution — those students won't find a URL on their own. That's why the school channel is the plan: one teacher with one link reaches a whole classroom, including every kid who would never have sought out a speaking coach.

### "Does it work in Bahasa Malaysia / other languages?"
> Today the curriculum and judging are in English — which is honest to the immediate pain point, since the interviews and pitches our users are preparing for are mostly English. The architecture isn't English-bound: transcription is multilingual and the rubric scores structure and persuasion, not Englishness. BM support is a real roadmap item, and a school pilot is exactly where we'd build it, with teachers checking the judging quality.

## The personal / team cluster

### "Why did YOU build this? Why this problem?"
> *(Fill in live — this must be yours, not scripted. The strongest version is one honest sentence about a moment you or someone near you froze when speaking mattered. Judges remember the moment, not the metrics.)*

### "What did you learn building this that you didn't expect?"
> That the hard part of an AI product isn't the AI — it's everything around the moment of practice: getting someone to actually press record, making feedback land as encouragement instead of judgment, making them come back tomorrow. We rebuilt onboarding completely when we saw people dropping off before ever speaking once. The model was never the bottleneck; human hesitation was.

### "What's the biggest risk to this project?"
> Not competitors and not technology — distribution. A free product with no marketing budget reaches people slowly, and the students who need it most are the least likely to stumble onto it. That's why every part of our next step is about the school channel: one adoption decision that delivers thirty learners at once. We'd rather name that risk than pretend virality is a strategy.

### "If a big tech company launches this feature tomorrow, what happens?"
> They'd validate the market, and they'd build it as a feature — a button inside someone else's product, monetized, generic. We're a *place*: a curriculum, a ladder you climb, a rank you defend, friends to beat. Duolingo didn't lose when Google Translate got good, because Translate solves translation and Duolingo solves *becoming someone who speaks*. Same shape here.

### "Where is this in five years, realistically?"
> Realistic version: the default speaking-practice tool in a few hundred schools, the way typing tutors were once standard — a skill lab any teacher can assign, free for every student, with institutions funding the dashboards on top. The unrealistic version is the same sentence with bigger numbers, and we'd rather earn the small version first.

### "If you don't win today, was this worth it?"
> It already was. The product exists, strangers use it without being asked, and we know more about shipping a real AI product than any classroom could have taught us. The prize accelerates the plan; it isn't the plan.
